What Eli Lilly's Numbers Reveal About Pharma's Biggest Untapped Opportunity

A close reading of one company's annual report offers the whole industry a roadmap — one that points toward more patients served, and stronger profits to show for it.
By Munashe Machoko
Tuck MBA | Healthcare, Data Analytics, and Finance Enthusiast
Published April 9, 2026
GLP-1 drugs moved from niche diabetes therapies to mass-market demand after proving highly efficacious in weight management—pricing, not science, became the constraint.

The U.S. pharmaceutical industry operates in one of the most complex business environments on earth. It shoulders enormous R&D risk, navigates a labyrinthine regulatory landscape, and funds the clinical failures of tomorrow with the commercial successes of today. These are not trivial constraints, and anyone who reduces the drug pricing debate to simple villainy has not spent much time understanding how medicines actually reach patients.

With that context firmly in place, consider what Eli Lilly's 2025 annual report quietly revealed: that one of the most powerful growth levers available to the industry had been sitting largely unexploited — and that accessing it required rethinking a deeply ingrained assumption about how to price drugs in markets with very different kinds of buyers.

Page 49 of Lilly's 10-K contains numbers worth pausing on. Mounjaro, the company's flagship GLP-1 therapy, saw revenues climb 99 percent year over year. Zepbound, its obesity-indicated counterpart, posted revenue growth of 175 percent. Both gains were driven almost entirely by volume — meaning more patients accessing treatment, not higher prices per patient. Net profits followed, rising 95 percent.

The economic mechanism behind these results is well-established, even if it is underappreciated in industry practice. GLP-1 drugs serve two meaningfully distinct patient populations — and it is critical to understand what distinguishes them before drawing any pricing conclusions. People managing Type 2 diabetes depend on these therapies for their health, and crucially, most are covered by insurance. That coverage structure means their out-of-pocket costs are largely insulated from list price changes; the insurer absorbs the bulk of the cost. For these patients, demand is relatively inelastic not because they are being exploited, but because the insurance system functions as it is designed to: absorbing cost and enabling access. The argument here is emphatically not that companies should charge diabetic patients more because they have no choice. Rather, it is that the existing insurance architecture already handles their access, so list price adjustments directed at the uninsured population do not meaningfully change what insured patients pay at the pharmacy counter.

People seeking treatment for obesity, by contrast, face a very different insurance landscape: many plans do not cover GLP-1s for weight management, leaving patients to pay out of pocket. For this population, demand is highly elastic — price is the primary determinant of access. These are the patients who, at a $1,000 monthly list price, simply go without.

In elastic markets, the mathematics of pricing work in a counterintuitive but well-documented way. When a producer with meaningful market power — as Lilly has in this therapeutic category — reduces prices, the proportional increase in demand exceeds the proportional price reduction. Volume surges. And for a company with the manufacturing scale and operational leverage of a large pharmaceutical firm, that volume surge translates directly to the bottom line. This is not new theory. It is the foundational logic of market segmentation and price discrimination, and it has been sitting in economics textbooks for generations.

The practical mechanism Lilly used to unlock this value was LillyDirect, a direct-to-consumer platform that bypasses the traditional distribution chain. At a list price of roughly $1,000 per month, GLP-1 therapies were effectively inaccessible to uninsured or underinsured patients in the obesity market. Through LillyDirect, that price fell to $245 per month — and a previously excluded population entered the market in significant numbers. The platform serves a dual strategic purpose: it compresses the gap between manufacturer and patient, and it generates direct demand data that improves future pricing and commercial decisions.

It is worth noting that the external context here mattered. The current administration's pursuit of a Most Favored Nation pricing policy prompted negotiations between government and industry that accelerated these price adjustments. The fact that lower prices coincided with, rather than undermined, Lilly's profit growth was not the outcome the industry predicted — and that gap between prediction and result is itself informative. It suggests that the intuition guiding some pricing decisions may be less well-calibrated to actual demand conditions than the tools now available to the industry would permit.

None of this is to suggest that pharmaceutical pricing is straightforward, or that a single case study resolves the broader policy debate. The rebate system, pharmacy benefit managers, formulary design, and payer negotiations all shape the prices patients actually pay in ways that defy simple summary. R&D economics remain genuinely difficult: the cost of developing a new molecular entity has risen steadily for decades, and the revenue from successful drugs must cross-subsidize a long trail of clinical failures. These structural realities constrain what any pricing strategy can accomplish.

But structural complexity is distinct from analytical imprecision. The industry now has access to demand-modeling tools, real-time patient data, and granular cost accounting of a sophistication that previous generations of executives could not have imagined. The question worth asking — seriously, inside boardrooms and not only in policy hearings — is whether those tools are being fully deployed in pricing decisions, particularly for drugs that serve populations with very different price sensitivities.

Lilly's results suggest the returns to that analysis can be substantial. A framework that segments markets rigorously, matches price to elasticity in each, and invests in distribution channels that close the gap between list price and patient cost can simultaneously expand access and strengthen financial performance. That is not a forced trade-off between social good and shareholder value. It is, in the terms that matter most to a business, a better strategy.

The pharmaceutical industry has earned, through decades of genuine scientific achievement, the right to be taken seriously as a partner in solving health challenges — not just as a target of political frustration. Exercising that standing means engaging rigorously with the economics of access, not just the economics of exclusivity. The companies that do so earliest will find, as Lilly did, that the market was larger than their pricing assumed.

At its core, pricing is the mechanism through which markets allocate resources and signal value. In pharmaceuticals, getting that mechanism right is not only good business — it is the clearest path toward aligning commercial success with the mission that gives the industry its social license in the first place.

Lilly's 2025 numbers are a proof of concept. The playbook is there for any company willing to run it.
